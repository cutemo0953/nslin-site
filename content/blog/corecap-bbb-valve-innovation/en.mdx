## The Problem: Clogged Valves, Slow Inflation, and a Tool You Keep Losing

### Sealant clogs the valve over time

Riders who run tubeless tires deal with the same set of frustrations, and they all trace back to one component: the valve core. Tubeless systems rely on liquid sealant to self-heal small punctures, but that sealant contains latex particles that gradually build up inside the valve, restricting airflow. Over time, each pump stroke moves less air, and inflation gets noticeably slower.

### Adding sealant means carrying a separate tool

Topping off sealant requires removing the valve core, which means carrying a dedicated valve core tool — a tiny piece that is easy to lose and hard to find when you actually need it. None of these are dealbreakers on their own, but for riders who maintain their own tires, they are a recurring annoyance that adds friction to every sealant top-up and every inflation.

## The Insight: Why Existing Solutions Don't Actually Fix This

### Other caps just push down the core

There are already "tool-free" valve caps on the market — caps that let you inflate without removing a separate cover. But look at how they actually work: the cap **pushes down on the existing valve core** to open the air passage. The core stays inside the stem.

### The core is still in the way

A standard valve core is a spring-loaded plunger assembly that sits inside the stem, taking up a significant portion of the internal cross-section. No matter how clever the cap design, as long as the core occupies that space, airflow has a hard ceiling. Sealant residue accumulates around the core even faster, because the narrow gaps act like traps. These tool-free designs improve convenience, but they do not touch the root cause of restricted flow.

## The Solution: Move the Valve Into the Cap

### A new mechanism, built from scratch

BBB CoreCap, designed and manufactured by N.S.-LIN (奕道實業) in Tainan, Taiwan, and exclusively distributed by <a href="https://bbbcycling.com/en_en/corecap" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">BBB Cycling</a>, takes a fundamentally different approach. **CoreCap moves the check-valve mechanism into the cap itself.** This patented design (patent pending) means the valve stem does not need a traditional valve core inside it — pull it out and leave it out.

### The stem interior is completely clear

The result: no spring, no plunger, nothing blocking airflow. The air channel cross-section is maximized, inflation is significantly faster, and even if sealant residue builds up, the open bore is far harder to clog than a core-obstructed passage.

### The cap is the tool

Need to add sealant? Unthread the cap, use it to extract the core, done. No separate tool to carry or lose. One component solves two problems: higher airflow because the core is gone from the stem, and no extra tool because the cap is the removal tool. This is not a refinement of an existing design — it is a new product architecture, engineered from scratch.

## The Proof: Why This Is Harder Than It Sounds

Moving the valve mechanism into a cap sounds straightforward. Making it work reliably at production volume — across thousands of units, thousands of use cycles, and years of exposure to sealant, weather, and road abuse — is where the engineering gets serious.

### The airflow-strength-seal triangle

Removing the valve core frees up internal space, but pushing flow rates even higher still requires thinning the stem walls (the outer diameter is fixed by the rim hole standard). Thinner walls create competing constraints: the stem takes repeated force from pump heads, compression from the rim lock nut, and constant vibration — any deformation risks thread failure or seal misalignment.

Every sealing surface becomes more demanding as walls thin: the base joint, the new valve mechanism in the cap, the lock nut interface, each is a potential leak path. **Airflow, wall strength, and long-term seal form an engineering triangle.** CoreCap had to find the optimal balance across all three, using a mechanism with no production history to reference.

### The multi-function cap

The cap itself is a precision challenge. A single component must house external threads that mate securely with the valve stem without loosening from vibration, an internal profile that grips the valve core for extraction, and the check-valve mechanism that provides the one-way seal function. These functional surfaces sit in close proximity, and the dimensional relationships between them determine whether the product works. Reliable performance after thousands of cycles requires tight tolerances — the kind that rule out most mass-production methods.

### Why CNC is the only option

CoreCap is a ground-up design. Every CNC program and fixture was developed from scratch for a mechanism that did not exist before. Injection molding cannot hold the thread precision — mold shrinkage kills the core-gripping function. Die-casting develops fatigue cracks under cyclic load. Stamping cannot produce the cap's internal geometry.

The entire line is **CNC-turned from 6061-T6 aluminum alloy**: tight wall thickness control balancing internal volume against structural margin, thread concentricity from a single setup that eliminates runout from secondary fixturing, and sealing surface finish at roughness levels substantially better than cast or molded parts. The material matters too — 6061-T6 resists the corrosive environment of tubeless sealant (latex particles, ammonia), offers strong fatigue life at reduced wall thickness, and adds no perceptible weight.

### 168+ hours of airtight validation

A novel mechanism with no production track record demands rigorous seal testing. N.S.-LIN does not rely on quick leak-down checks. **Samples from every production batch undergo a static pressure hold exceeding one full week (168+ hours), with zero measurable pressure decay required to pass.** The 168-hour figure is the minimum, not the target — actual durations often run longer.

The criterion is absolute: not "below threshold," but no detectable pressure drop within instrument resolution. This is a full-system test covering the entire assembled valve, not just one seal point. The reasoning is specific to tubeless: when a tubeless valve leaks, sealant wicks into the leak path and cures unevenly, creating residue that accelerates seal degradation. "Almost airtight" and "fully airtight" are not degrees of the same thing — they are different quality tiers.

### Six anodized colors

Black, Blue, Bronze, Gold, Red, and Purple. Anodizing grows a dense aluminum oxide layer (Al₂O₃, 10-25 μm) that is significantly harder (HV 300-400) than the base metal (HV 95-110). Unlike paint, it is a chemical conversion of the surface — it will not chip, peel, or delaminate.

## iF Design Award

CoreCap earned an **iF Design Award**, one of the most respected international industrial design competitions. The jury recognized its integration of form and function — solving a real user problem with fewer parts, not more. **The best valve innovation is not about adding features. It is about removing unnecessary components.**
